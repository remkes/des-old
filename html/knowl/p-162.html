The other substitution I want to present is for a class of DEs called Bernoulli equations. These equations are nearly linear, but they have an extra \(y^n\) term. They are written in a form very similar to the form for linear DEs.

\begin{equation*} \frac{dy}{dt} + P(t) y = Q(t) y^n\text{.} \end{equation*}

The substitution that simplifies a Bernoulli equation is \(v = y^{1-n}\text{.}\) Be careful: this is \(y^{1-n}\text{,}\) not \(y^{n-1}\text{.}\) It's a very easy mistake to get this exponent wrong. I transform the DE by looking at the following derivations.

\begin{align*} \frac{dv}{dt} \amp = (1-n) y^{1-n-1} \frac{dy}{dt} = (1-n)y^{-n} \frac{dy}{dt}\\ \amp = (1-n)y^{-n} \left( Q(t) y^n - P(t) y \right) \\ \amp = (1-n) Q(t) - (1-n) P(t) y^{1-n}\\ \amp = (1-n) Q(t) - (1-n) P(t) v\\ \frac{dv}{dt} + (1-n)v P(t) \amp = (1-n) Q(t) \end{align*}

This is now a linear equation in \(v\text{.}\) I can solve it as a linear equation in \(v\) and then use the reverse substitution to get back to \(y\text{.}\)
